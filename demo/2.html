First principles selection and design of mid-IR nonlinear optical halide crystals†

Abstract

The optical properties of several distinct series of nonlinear optical (NLO) halide crystals with potential applications in the mid-infrared (mid-IR) spectral region have been investigated systematically using a first-principles computational methodology. The crystals have different NLO-active units [MXk] (M = center cation, X = halide anion, and k = 6, 4, 3 or 2) and by combining atomic-level analysis with optical design considerations, their prospects for mid-IR applications are evaluated. The preferred microscopic structural units, which result in a relatively large NLO response and high laser damage threshold, are proposed. The study provides first principles guidance in the selection and design of novel halide crystals for mid-IR NLO applications.

1. Introduction

Second-order nonlinear optical (NLO) materials are playing an important role in many advanced scientific and technological areas.1–6 Numerous NLO crystals used in the ultraviolet (UV) and visible regions have been discovered in the last few decades including KH2PO4 (KDP),7 KTiOPO4 (KTP),8 β-BaB2O4 (BBO),9 LiB3O5 (LBO)10 and LiNbO3 (LN).11 The demands of high-power laser harmonic generation in these spectral regions are almost fully satisfied using these crystals. However, in contrast, NLO materials currently used in the mid-infrared (mid-IR) region, including AgGaS2,12 AgGaSe2,13 ZnGeP2,14 and LiBC215,16 (B = Al, Ga, In; C = S, Se, Te), are inadequate for applications principally because of their low laser damage threshold (LDT).17 Therefore, the search for new mid-IR NLO crystals that perform well, and have a high LDT, has become one of the greatest challenges in NLO materials science and laser technology.

It is known that a high LDT in a NLO crystal usually corresponds to the large energy band gap (Eg),18 yet increasing the band gap would result in a decrease in the second-harmonic generation (SHG) coefficients (dij).19 Therefore achieving a balance between having a large band gap and strong SHG effects (Egvs. dij) is the key issue in the search and design of mid-IR NLO materials. In general, a good mid-IR NLO material should satisfy the following requirements in terms of the constituent elements and structural features: (i) in order to obtain a wide IR transparent window, the crystal should be composed of relatively heavy elements; (ii) in order to increase the crystal band gap (preferably larger than 3.0 eV, see Table S1 and Fig. S1 in the ESI†), the cations and anions should have a large difference in electronegativity so that strongly ionic chemical bonds are formed, which would promote the tight binding of valence electrons around the anions and result in wide band gaps; and (iii) in order to enhance the SHG effects (usually 10 times larger than the effective SHG coefficient deff of KDP, see Formula S1 in the ESI†), the optically active structural units should be arranged in an additive manner with large microscopic second-order susceptibilities, for example, second-order Jahn–Teller (SOJT) distortions at d0 or d10 metal centers,20–22 distorted polyhedra with stereochemically active lone electron pairs,23,24 or noncentrosymmetric planes with π–orbitals.25,26 At the same time, the optical anisotropy of the crystal should be large so that the birefringence is sufficient to satisfy the phase-matching condition for harmonic generation in the mid-IR region.

Over the past two decades, many studies have focused on oxide and chalcogenide NLO crystals. Oxide-based crystals have high-power applications generally in the UV and visible regions because of their reasonable NLO coefficients and high LDT in the UV to near-IR range (0.16–3.0 μm). However, almost all of them have high optical absorption in the mid-wave infrared (3–5 μm and 8–12 μm) due to the intrinsic vibration energy of the oxide groups, so their applications in the mid-IR region are restricted. On the other hand, many chalcogenide (including S, Se and Te) crystals possess wide IR transparent windows (0.4–15 μm) and large NLO coefficients. However, their relatively narrow band gaps (typically Eg < 3 eV) seriously limit the LDT for harmonic generation in these crystals27 although recently materials such as BaGa4S728 and Li2Ga2GeS2,29 which have relatively large energy gaps of 3.54 eV and 3.65 eV respectively, have been synthesized.

In recent years, in order to explore wide band gap IR NLO crystals, attention has been drawn to halide-based materials since they could improve the LDT according to the second requirement above. Many NLO halide crystals having wide IR transmission, reasonable SHG coefficients and relatively high LDT have been discovered, including CsGeX3 (X = Cl, Br, I),30–32 CsCdBr3,33 Tl4HgI6,34 Tl3PbBr5,35 Cs2Hg3I8,36 SbF3,37 NaSb3F10,38 KIO2F2,39 HgBr2,40 Hg2BrI3,41 Hg2Br3I42 and Cs2HgCl2I2,43 and are considered to be the most promising materials for mid-IR applications. In particular, the LDT of CsGeCl3,30 HgBr240 and NaSb3F1038 can reach 200 MW cm−2, 300 MW cm−2 and 1.3 GW cm−2, respectively, which is much larger than that of AgGaS2 (25 MW cm−2),44 AgGaSe2 (30 MW cm−2),45 and LiInS2 (67 MW cm−2).46 However, an understanding of the relationship between the atomic/electronic structure and optical properties in the NLO halides is still lacking and this inevitably hinders their development and application.

In this work, first-principles calculations at the atomic level are used to systematically investigate the linear and nonlinear optical properties of IR halide crystals with representative optically active structural units. Enhancement of the band gap and the nonlinear effect in these materials is discussed using electronic structure methods. The structural suitability of the halide crystals as good mid-IR NLO materials is evaluated by cataloguing the active NLO units. The preferred structural units, which result in a relatively large NLO response and high LDT, are then proposed.

2. Computational methods

In the present first principles calculations, the plane-wave pseudopotential method47 based on density functional theory48 is used. The CASTEP program49 is employed to perform the geometry optimizations and the electronic structure calculations. Optimized norm-conserving pseudopotentials of the Kleinman–Bylander form50–52 are used for the elements to ensure a small plane-wave basis set without compromising the accuracy of the results. A kinetic energy cutoff of 900 eV is chosen together with Monkhorst–Pack k-point meshes53 spanning less than 0.04 per Å3 in the Brillouin zone. Convergence tests show that the above computational parameters are sufficiently accurate for present purposes.

The mid-IR NLO halides studied include those upon which measurements have already been made, i.e. CsGeX3 (X = Cl, Br, I), CsCdBr3, Cs2Hg3I8, SbF3, NaSb3F10 and HgBr2. These crystals, which exhibit various structures and symmetries, can be classified into four types, as follows, according to their fundamental structural units [MXk], where M is the central metal cation, X is the halide anion and k = 6, 4, 3, and 2: (i) [MX6] distorted octahedra such as [GeX6] in CsGeX3 (X = Cl, Br, I) (Fig. 1(a)) and [CdBr6] in CsCdBr3 (Fig. 1(b)). The former are connected to each other by sharing the corner halide anion, similar to the [TiO6] units in BaTiO354,55 or the [NbO6] units in KNbO3,55 while the latter are connected to each other by sharing edges. In both cases, the distorted directions in the octahedra are parallel, which is actually beneficial for the superposition of the microscopic second-order susceptibility; (ii) [MX4] units such as the [HgI4] tetrahedra in Cs2Hg3I8 (Fig. 1(c)), in which the distorted Hg-centered tetrahedra are distributed in layers and the edges linked by the corner halides are approximately parallel; (iii) [MX3] triangular-pyramids such as the [SbF3] units in NaSb3F10 and SbF3 (Fig. 1(d)). Both units have stereoactive lone pairs of electrons located around the Sb3+ ions (see Fig. S2 in the ESI†).56 The main structural difference is that in the former crystal the [SbF3] units are arranged in the a–b plane, while in the latter crystal they are aligned along the c-axis; and (iv) [MX2] one-dimensional strings such as the [HgBr2] units in HgBr2 (Fig. 1(e)), in which the linear units are isolated while their orientation could be divided into two categories and in each category the units are parallel to one another.


Fig. 1 Structures containing [MXk] units in (a) CsGeX3 (X = Cl, Br, I), (b) CsCdBr3, (c) Cs2Hg3I8, (d) NaSb3F10 (left) and SbF3 (right), and (e) HgBr2.

In order to understand the influence of the halogen ions on the optical properties, a series of hypothetical halide crystals are also constructed by substituting one type of halogen ion with another in the unit cell of some of the known halides (e.g. replacing the F atoms in NaSb3F10 by Cl, Br, or I to construct the hypothetical crystals NaSb3Cl10, NaSb3Br10, and NaSb3I10 respectively). The cell parameters and the atomic positions in the unit cells of both the real crystals and the hypothetical crystals are fully optimized using the quasi-Newton method.57 The convergence thresholds between optimization cycles for energy change, maximum force, maximum stress, and maximum displacement are set as 10−5 eV per atom, 0.03 eV per Å, 0.05 GPa, and 0.001 Å, respectively. The optimization terminates when all of these criteria are satisfied, and the crystallographic data of the optimized crystals including CsGeX3, Cs2Hg3X8, NaSb3X10 and SbX3 (X = F, Cl, Br, I) are listed in Table S2 in the ESI.† The electronic structures in these halides are then calculated from the relaxed geometries.

Based on the electronic structures, the imaginary part of the dielectric function can be calculated from the electronic transition between the occupied and unoccupied states caused by the interaction with photons, and the real part of the dielectric function is determined using the Kramers–Kronig transform, from which the refractive indices (and the birefringence) are obtained.58 Furthermore, the second-order susceptibility χ(2), i.e., the SHG coefficient dij, is calculated using an expression developed previously by Lin at al.59 Consequently, the corresponding powder SHG (PSHG) effect can also be obtained using the Kurtz–Perry method.60

It is well known that DFT calculations that use the local density approximation (LDA)61 for the exchange-correlation (XC) functional always underestimate the energy band gap of a crystal due to the discontinuous behavior of the XC energy. One way of dealing with the band gap problem is to use the screened exchange LDA (sX-LDA)62 method. Table 1 lists the experimental and calculated optical energy band gaps of the representative halides, for which measurements have been made, using different XC functionals including PBE063 which is a hybrid form of the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) generalized gradient approximation (GGA) functional. It is seen that the sX-LDA values are indeed much better than those using the LDA or PBE0 functionals, especially for the crystals with band gaps less than 4.2 eV. Therefore, the sX-LDA method is employed to predict the band gaps of the hypothetical crystals. However, for the optical property calculations, a scissors operator,64,65 which is the difference between the experimental value and LDA-calculated value, is adopted to shift up all the conduction bands to agree with the measured band gap. This treatment is based on our previous studies,66 which concluded that although the sX-LDA method is good at predicting the UV-edge wavelength, the resulting calculated electronic band structures and optical properties are not well reproduced compared to the scissors-corrected LDA functional. Thus, for the hypothetical crystals, the scissors operator is set as the difference between sX-LDA and LDA band gaps.

3. Results and discussion

Fig. 2 shows the electronic partial density of states (PDOS) for the representative halides CsGeX3 (X = Cl, Br, I), CsCdBr3, Cs2Hg3I8, SbF3, NaSb3F10, and HgBr2 (their band structures are displayed in Fig. S3 of the ESI†). It is found that for all the crystals the inner orbitals of the A-site cations, e.g., Cs or Na, and the s orbitals of the anions F, Cl, Br or I are strongly localized deep in the valence band (VB). They have a negligible influence on electronic states close to the band gap. The top of the VB is mainly composed of the p orbitals of the halide anions, e.g., F 2p orbitals occupying the energy range −7 to 0 eV in NaSb3F10 and SbF3, Br 3p orbitals occupying the range −6 to 0 eV in CsGeBr3 and −4 to 0 eV in CsCdBr3 and HgBr2. The orbitals of the central cations, such as Ge, Cd, Hg and Sb, in the [MXk] anionic units, make only a small contribution to the top of the valence band; their main contribution is to the bottom of the conduction band (CB). It is seen that the contribution from the halide anions to the CB minimum increases as the ionic radius increases. It should be emphasized that the states on both sides of the band gap are mainly composed of orbitals from the [MXk] anionic units, i.e. nearly nothing from the A-site cations (Cs+ or Na+). Since the optical response of a crystal in the visible-IR region originates mainly from electronic transitions between the VB and CB states, especially between those close to the band gap, caused by the interaction with photons, the structure and composition of the [MXk] anionic units determine the optical properties of the mid-IR halides, in accordance with the anionic group theory proposed by Chen67 for ultraviolet NLO crystals.
Fig. 2 Partial density of states (PDOS) projected on the constituent atoms in (a) CsGeCl3, (b) CsGeBr3, (c) CsGeI3, (d) CsCdBr3, (e) Cs2Hg3I8, (f) NaSb3F10, (g),SbF3 and (h) HgBr2. The dashed lines indicate the valence band maximum.

Based on the above analysis, the optical properties of the crystals studied (including the hypothetical crystals), i.e., the calculated SHG coefficients, refractive indices n and birefringence Δn, as well as the experimental data, are tabulated according to the different values of k in the [MXk] units. The calculated optical properties are in good agreement with the available experimental values, which clearly demonstrates the validity of the plane-wave pseudopotential method for the present studies.

Table 2 lists the experimental and calculated optical properties of the crystals containing [MX6] units. It is clear that the birefringence values for CsGeX3 (X = F, Cl, Br, I) and CsCdBr3 are very small (Δn < 0.01), although they increase slightly with respect to the radius of the halide anions. By comparison, the birefringence of oxide crystals containing similar octahedral units is much larger. For instance, Δn for BaTiO3 and KNbO3 is 0.04 and 0.14, respectively.54,68 This is mainly because the charge densities are more delocalized in the [MO6] octahedra compared with those in the [GeX6] and [CdBr6] octahedra (see Fig. S4 in the ESI†), which results in a much larger optical anisotropy in the former microscopic units. Furthermore, the crystal symmetry of CsGeX3 and CsCdBr3 is rhombohedral, which is almost cubic (even though three Ge-X bond-lengths are much shorter than the rest in the [GeX6] octahedra), and the optical birefringence becomes very small. However, the parallel arrangement of the [MX6] units makes the overall SHG effect in the crystals relatively large, since the measured NLO coefficients of CsCdBr3, CsGeCl3 and CsGeBr3 are about 2, 5 and 10 times larger respectively than that of KDP.

Since the distorted [HgX4] tetrahedra distribute in a layered arrangement, the structure exhibits strong anisotropy so that the birefringence is relatively large (Δn > 0.06). Meanwhile, the polarized directions which are approximately parallel cause a strong SHG response, and the SHG coefficients increase as the ionic radius of the halide element increases. It is noted that the nonparallel polarization component of the [MX4] arrangement can result in a significant decrease in the NLO effect. For instance, in the halide crystal Cs2HgI2Cl2,43 the tetrahedral [HgI2Cl2] units are connected in such a way that the distorted orientation is partly compensated and the SHG effect is only ∼1 × KDP.

The calculated and experimental optical properties of the crystals containing the [SbX3] units, which are triangular-pyramids, are listed in Table 4. Since the strongly distorted lone electron pairs in the [SbX3] units are arranged in parallel (see Fig. S2 in the ESI†), all these crystals exhibit very large optical anisotropy. The NaSb3F10 and SbF3 crystals even possess the largest birefringence (Δn > 0.1) of all the fluorides experimentally discovered. Moreover, the triangular-pyramidal units result in a strong NLO response, which can be seen from the experimental efficiencies of NaSb3F10 and SbF3 (∼3 and 6 × KDP) and the calculated values for NaSb3Br10 and SbCl3 (∼10 and 15 × KDP).

If they are in a parallel linear arrangement, the [MX2] one-dimensional strings are expected to be active units with large optical anisotropy. Indeed HgBr2, which consists of linear [HgBr2] chains, has a large birefringence (Δn ∼ 0.238 calculated at 1064 nm) and strong SHG effect (∼10 × KDP).40 Moreover, recently discovered one-dimensional materials also have similar NLO properties to HgBr2 and show promise for applications in the IR region.42

From the above results, it is clear that most of the crystals studied have enough optical birefringence to achieve the required phase-matching condition in the mid-IR region except those with the [MX6] unit where Δn < 0.01. More importantly, the SHG response tends to increase as k decreases provided that the [MXk] units are arranged in parallel. Furthermore as the electronegativity of the halide element decreases from F to Cl, Br, and I, the energy band gap Eg decreases but the SHG coefficients dij increase (see Fig. S5 in the ESI†). This confirms that seeking a balance between Eg and dij is indeed a key issue in finding good mid-IR NLO crystals. The distribution of band gaps with respect to the SHG effect for all the crystals studied is plotted in Fig. 3, where the requirement for being a good mid-IR NLO crystal, i.e., band gap greater than 3.0 eV and SHG effect larger than 10 × KDP, is shown by the shaded region. It is found that NaSb3F10 possesses the largest energy band gap (∼5.5 eV) but a small SHG effect (∼2 × KDP), while SbI3 has the strongest SHG effect (>100 × KDP) but a small energy band gap (∼1.5 eV). For the other fluoride and iodide crystals similar results are obtained and hence they are not suitable candidates for mid-IR NLO applications. Furthermore, applications using the [MX6] halide crystals are also limited by either weak SHG effects or small birefringence. Therefore, the crystals satisfying the good mid-IR NLO requirements are those containing [MX4], [MX3] or [MX2] units and X = Cl or Br, e.g., Cs2Hg3Cl8 with [MX4] units, SbCl3, SbBr3 and NaSb3Br10 with [MX3] units and HgBr2 with [MX2] units. It is seen that some of these crystals are hypothetical but if it were possible to synthesize them it is predicted that they would make promising mid-IR NLO materials. For instance, Cs2Hg3Cl8 exhibits not only a large anisotropic optical response (SHG effect ∼20 × KDP and birefringence ∼0.06) but also a considerable band gap (∼3.4 eV). More importantly, this crystal is kinetically stable as demonstrated by first-principles phonon calculations, using the linear response method described in our previous studies on carbonate NLO crystals69 (for the phonon spectrum see Fig. S6 in the ESI†). This means that this crystal could be synthesized, and the relevant experimental studies are underway. Finally it should be emphasized that, in practice, the magnitude of the birefringence and SHG coefficients is also closely related to the choice of the central M cations in the [MXk] units.

4. Conclusion

The SHG effects and LDT significantly affect the performance of NLO crystals in the mid-IR spectral region. The former is determined by the structural distortion and arrangement of the NLO-active unit, while the latter mainly by the energy band gap. It is desirable to maximize both properties. However, since the SHG effects decrease with increasing band gap, a compromise between the structural and electronic characteristics of a candidate material must be made. In this work, using a first principles density functional approach, the microscopic properties of several distinct series of halide crystals were systematically investigated to determine their potential as good mid-IR NLO materials. Based on the calculated electronic structures, the linear and nonlinear optical properties of halide crystals containing [MXk] units (M = center cation, X = halide anion, and k = 6, 4, 3 or 2) were determined and found to be in good agreement with available experimental data. It is seen that the optical response of these crystals is determined mainly by the [MXk] anionic units. The studies reveal that octahedral [MX6] units might not be favored as active units for mid-IR NLO halide crystals mainly due to the nearly isotropic optical birefringence. To obtain good NLO crystals containing tetrahedral [MX4] units, the microscopic units must to be spatially aligned in a preferred orientation and should not contain fluorine or iodine. On the other hand, the triangular-pyramidal [MX3] units and the linear [MX2] chains, where X = Cl or/and Br, are the preferred structural units for good mid-IR NLO crystals. It is thought that this atomic level insight will be significant in the search and design of new wide-gap NLO crystals in the mid-IR spectral region. In addition by varying the central M cation in the [MXk] units, further promising NLO crystals could be found with good performance characteristics in the mid-IR spectral region.
