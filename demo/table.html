Influence of excited state aromaticity in the lowest excited singlet states of fulvene derivatives†

Martin Rosenberg a, Henrik Ottosson b and Kristine Kilså *a
aDepartment of Chemistry, University of Copenhagen, Universitetsparken 5, DK-2100 Copenhagen Ø, Denmark. E-mail: kkj@nano.ku.dk
bDepartment of Biochemistry and Organic Chemistry, Uppsala University, Box 576, 751 23 Uppsala, Sweden

Received 8th December 2010 , Accepted 27th May 2011

First published on 21st June 2011

Abstract

The absorption spectra and excited state dipole moments of four differently substituted fulvenes have been investigated both experimentally and computationally. The results reveal that the excited state dipole moment of fulvenes reverses in the first excited singlet state when compared to the ground state. The oppositely polarized electron density distributions, which dominate the ground state and the first excited singlet state of fulvenes, respectively, reflect the reversed π-electron counting rules for aromaticity in the two states (4n + 2 vs. 4n, respectively). The results show that substituents indeed influence the polarity of fulvenes in the two states, however, cooperative interactions between the substituents and the fulvene moiety are most pronounced in the ground state.

Introduction

Fulvene is a weakly dipolar isomer of benzene which has been investigated with a variety of spectroscopic techniques, for instance, absorption spectroscopy for extensive studies of the electronic spectrum,1–4photoelectron spectroscopy to map the orbital sequence,5 and more recently, electron-energy-loss spectroscopy to locate and identify the singlet and triplet excited states.6 The ground (S0) state dipole moment of fulvenes has been measured to be 0.42 D, and is rationalized by an electron density distribution corresponding to a dipolar resonance structure with a negative charge located in the ring moiety and a positive charge positioned at the exocyclic C-atom.2,7 Substitution by alkyl or aryl groups at the exocyclic position has shown to increase the dipole moment by ∼1 D,8 while amino substituents have a more considerable impact on the dipole moment, borne out by a measured dipole moment of 6-dimethylaminofulvene of 4.5 D.9 These results indicate that the electron density is shifted towards the ring if the electron donating effect of the exocyclic substituents is increased.10,11 This result has further been supported by the results from quantum chemical calculations on fulvenes with different exocyclic substituents.12–15

Interestingly, computational studies also reveal that the dipole moment of fulvenes reverses relative to the ground state (S0 state) when the compound is excited to the lowest (π,π*) excited triplet state (T1 state).16,17 We earlier argued that oppositely polarized electron density distributions are favorable in the two states as they correspond to resonance structures with different numbers of π-electrons located at the ring moiety,15,17 in accordance with Hückel's and Baird's contrasting rules for aromaticity/antiaromaticity in the S0 and T1 states, respectively (Fig. 1).18–22 The oppositely polarized electron density distributions are reflected in the effects of different substituents on the T1 state properties of fulvenes, and these effects are especially noteworthy for fulvenes substituted at the exocyclic position. In contrast to what applies for the S0 state, electron withdrawing groups at the exocyclic position enhance the T1 state dipole moment through increased importance of the T1 state aromatic resonance structure (Fig. 1), while electron donating groups reduce it,15 thus complying with the reversal of important resonance structures in the T1vs. S0 states.


Fig. 1 Resonance structure representation of the oppositely polarized electron density distributions that influence the S0 and T1 states of fulvenes. The directions of the calculated dipole moments in the two states indicate that the aromatic resonance structures are important for the description of the electron density distribution of fulvenes in the two states.

The reversed π-electron counting rules for aromaticity and antiaromaticity in the T1 state of annulenes have also been concluded to extend to the S1 state,23–30 and this was recently confirmed through NICS calculations at the CASSCF level by Karadakov.29,30 Indeed, the reversal in the S0 and S1 state aromaticity rules has earlier been utilized by Wan and co-workers as a tool to explain contrasting S0 and S1 state reactivities for several fluorene and suberene derivatives.31–46 With this background, it is natural to expect the S1 state dipole moment of fulvenes to have a direction similar to that found for the T1 state (Fig. 1). Results of computational studies have shown that this is indeed the case.16 The question is, however, to what extent the excited state aromaticity influences the S1 state? Singlet excited states are often more multiconfigurational than triplet excited states, and thus, the excited state aromaticity may play a smaller role in the S1 state than in the T1 state. On the other hand, singlet excited states are often more dipolar while triplet excited states are more diradical in character, and thus, an increased influence of the zwitterionic S1 state aromatic resonance structure may be observed for fulvenes. A detailed experimental investigation on how the substituents and solvent environment influence the electron density distribution in the S1 state of fulvenes is therefore crucial. The present investigation involves the symmetrically substituted fulvene derivatives 1–4 (Fig. 2), which were selected based on their stability and easily characterized spectroscopy.


Fig. 2 Fulvene compounds with different substituents at the ring moiety and at the exocyclic C-atom.

In addition to reveal whether there is a similar reversal in the S1 state polarity as in the T1 state, the study of these fulvenes could be useful for further applications of fulvenes in, e.g., organic electronics as it shows how to elaborate on the excited state properties not only through choice of substituents, but also through choice of the polarity of the surrounding medium. Finally, an experimental investigation of the influence of excited singlet state aromaticity in substituted fulvenes should be important from the viewpoint of probing the scope and limitations of the excited state aromaticity concept as such. So far, this concept is not used broadly by the photochemical research community for the rationalization of the excited state properties and processes, despite that it could have the same wide impact on photochemistry as S0 state aromaticity has on ground state properties and processes.

Experimental and computational methods

Spectroscopic methods

All experiments were performed at ambient temperature. Absorption spectra were recorded by using a Cary 50 Bio spectrophotometer (Varian Inc.) in a 1 cm quartz cuvette (Hellma) using the pure solvent as a baseline. All solvents, except dichloromethane and acetonitrile were purchased from LabScan and were of HPLC grade. Dichloromethane and acetonitrile were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and were of spectroscopic grade. All solvents were used as received.

Compounds

6,6-Dimethylfulvene (1) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and used as received. 1,2,3,4-Tetrachloro-6,6-dimethylfulvene (2), 1,2,3,4-tetrachloro-6,6-(N,N′-dipyrrolidino)fulvene (3), and 1,2,3,4-tetrachloro-6,6-dicyanofulvene (4) were synthesized as previously described.15

Computational methods

Calculations were performed with the GAUSSIAN 03 suite of programs.47 Recently, computational studies of fulvenic molecules using B3LYP/6-311+G(d,p) for geometry optimizations and TD-B3LYP/6-311+G(d,p) for the calculation of vertical excitation energies have shown to give results in agreement with experimental results.48 However, due to the lack of hydrogen atoms in 4, we excluded the polarization functions at hydrogen atoms for all compounds to achieve comparable results, i.e., all structures were geometry optimized at the B3LYP/6-311+G(d) level. The calculated structures are in good agreement with previous calculated structures.15 All compounds were optimized to minima on the potential energy surfaces, and this was verified through frequency calculations revealing no imaginary frequencies. Stabilities of the Kohn–Sham solutions were also checked.49 To simulate the non-specific solvent interactions of acetonitrile, the structures were used for re-optimization at the same level of theory using the polarizable continuum model in the integral-equation formalism (IEF-PCM) method.50–52 The IEF-PCM/B3LYP/6-311+G(d) optimized structures were then used to calculate vertical excitation energies at the IEF-PCM/TD-B3LYP/6-311+G(d) level.

The calculations of S0 and T1 state dipole moments for 1 were performed at the (U)OLYP/cc-pVTZ level,53–55 which has shown to give results comparable to high-level CASSCF results for a series of similar fulvene derivatives.15 Spherical cavity radii for 1–3 were calculated from the SCF density of the OLYP/cc-pVTZ optimized structures using the Monte-Carlo method.56 The radii used as cavity radii are 0.5 Å larger than the computed radii following the recommendations of GAUSSIAN 03.57

Results and discussion

The B3LYP/6-311+G(d) calculated gas phase structures of 1–4 showed to be in good agreement with previous reported structures.15 The structure of 3 is the most distorted from planarity due to the bulky substituents on the exocyclic C-atom. However, the CC–N–C dihedral angles are 18.5° and −152.3°, and the sum of the valence angles at N is 359.5°, revealing that the lone-pair electrons on the N-atoms can indeed be considered to be in conjugation with the fulvene π-system and that the N-atoms can be considered as sp2-hybridized. Both computational and experimental investigations of the absorption spectra of 1–4 will be presented in the following. First, we compare measured and calculated gas phase absorption spectra of non-substituted fulvenes with measured and calculated solution phase absorption spectra of 1–4. Then, the effects of the solvent environment and substitution of the fulvene moiety on the spectral positions in the absorption spectra will be discussed and related to the difference in the magnitude and direction of the dipole moment upon excitation.

For a comparison between the spectra of 1–4 and that of unsubstituted fulvenes we have presented the absorption spectra for 1–4 in Fig. 3 and listed the corresponding spectral data for these compounds in Table 1.3,15 The gas phase absorption spectrum of the parent fulvene is characterized by a broad, low intensity, and featureless absorption band which extends from 19600 cm−1 to 33300 cm−1, with a maximum at 27780 cm−1.3 This band has been assigned to the S0 (A1) → S1 (B2) transition.1,58 A second absorption band with a high intensity and vibrational fine structure covers the region from 37450 cm−1 to 48780 cm−1 with a maximum at 42550 cm−1.3 This band has been assigned to the S0 (A1) → S2 (A1) transition.1,58

The calculated results in Table 2 show that the transition at lowest energy for all compounds has the HOMO → LUMO configuration as the major configuration, and a small oscillator strength. The second lowest excitation energies of 1–4 correspond to transitions with the largest oscillator strength within the calculated excitation energies and are all mainly composed of the HOMO−1 → LUMO excited state configuration. The calculated excitation energies for the transitions mentioned above are overall in good agreement with the experimental excitation energies for the S0 → S1 and S0 → S2 transitions for 1–4 in Table 2. Although the lowest calculated excitation energy of 4 and the second lowest calculated excitation energy of 3 deviate by ∼25% from the experimental value, the remaining calculated energies only deviate by less than 8% when compared to the experimental values. Furthermore, the HOMO−1, HOMO and LUMO Kohn–Sham orbitals of 1–4 correlate with those of fulvenes recently calculated,48 and from Fig. 4 it is evident that the orbitals possess π-character so that the transitions are of (π,π*) character. It is therefore valid to compare these transitions of the various compounds.

As the compounds are non-emissive, only UV-Vis absorption spectra are measured. Compounds 1–3 show reasonable good linear dependencies between the excitation energies and f(ε) − f(n2) with fitted slopes, α, of 605.4 cm−1, 778.2 cm−1, and 3110 cm−1, respectively. However, 4 does not exhibit a correlation between the excitation energy and f(ε) − f(n2), which indicates that the dielectric continuum model is not sufficient to describe the interactions between solvent and 4. Therefore, information about the direction and magnitude of the dipole moment in the S1 state of 4 cannot be extracted from the experimental results. This may also explain the large deviation of the calculated excitation energy of the S0 → S1 transition, which is based on a similar dielectric continuum model. A discussion of the specific interactions between explicit solvent molecules and 4 is outside the scope of the present work.

The ground state dipole moments of 1–3 have previously been calculated as 1.99 D, 3.60 D, and 8.92 D, respectively, at the OLYP/TZ2P level for 1 and the UOLYP/cc-pVTZ level for 2 and 3. These methods have shown to give results in good agreement with the results of high level calculations (CASSCF) on related compounds.15 However, to be consistent we calculated the ground state dipole moment of 1 at the OLYP/cc-pVTZ level, which results in a minor change from 1.99 to 1.90 D. It should be mentioned that the dipole moment of 1 is known to be 1.44 D according to experiments.8 Yet, for consistency we used the calculated dipole moments of 1–3 in the interpretation of our results, as experimentally determined dipole moments of 2 and 3 have not been reported. The calculated ground state dipole moments of 1–3 all have the same direction with the negative charges located at the five-membered ring and the positive charge positioned at the exocyclic C-atom of the fulvene moiety.

From eqn (3) it can be deduced that a positive α-value is possible only if the numerical value of μe is smaller than μg. This can be obtained either as a decrease in magnitude or a sign (direction) reversal of the dipole moment. Thus, from the experimental results (Fig. 5) it is evident that μe < μg for 1–3. To evaluate the changes in the dipole moments of 1–3 upon excitation we use the calculated ground state dipole moments together with calculated cavity radii (a) of 4.31 Å, 4.91 Å and 5.37 Å for 1, 2, and 3, respectively ((U)OLYP/cc-pVTZ). From these values the S1 state dipole moments (μe,S) can be determined to be −3.17 D, −1.48 D, and −1.80 D for 1, 2 and 3, respectively (Table 4), i.e., indicating a reversal of the dipole moment direction in the S1 state for all three compounds.

Consequently, the dipolar electron density distributions with a negative charge located at the exocyclic carbon atom and a positive charge at the ring are indeed important in the S1 states of 1–3. This is in contrast to the S0 state, in which the oppositely polarized electron density distribution dominates as shown in Fig. 1. The differences in electron density distributions are illustrated with different resonance structures of 1–3 in Fig. 6.


Fig. 6 Illustration of the oppositely polarized electron density distributions by resonance structures of 1–3 in the S0 and S1 states, respectively, based on the information on the direction and magnitude of the dipole moments in the two states.

By comparing 2 to 1 it is seen that the incorporation of Cl-atoms in the 6,6-dimethylfulvene moiety results in an enhancement of the S0 state dipole moment. This we interpret as a consequence of the σ-inductive electron withdrawal effect of the Cl-atoms. In the following it is assumed that such σ-inductive effects are unchanged upon excitation, because the excitation to the S1 state involves the π-electrons, and thus, mainly π-polarization effects are responsible for the change in the electron density distribution upon excitation. The magnitudes of the difference between the S0 and S1 state dipole moments (|Δμge,S|) follow the trend: |Δμge,S(3)| > |Δμge,S(2)| ≈ |Δμge,S(1)|. As |Δμge,S(1)| and |Δμge,S(2)| are essentially equal (5.07 D vs. 5.08 D), it can be deduced that π-donation of the lone-pair electrons on the Cl-atoms (n(Cl)) towards ring has a minor effect on the change in electron density distribution upon excitation. However, the excitation energy for the S0 → S1 transition of 2 is less than that of 1. This can be explained by an interaction between n(Cl) and the π-electrons in the fulvene moiety. As the ring moiety is electron abundant in the S0 state this interaction will act destabilizing on the S0 state energy, while act stabilizing on the S1 state energy, as the ring is electron deficient in this state. This lowers the energy gap between the S0 and the S1 states in 2 when compared to 1 as observed experimentally.

The incorporation of N-atoms at the exocyclic position (3) has a huge effect on the magnitude of the S0 state dipole moment compared to 2 (8.92 D for 3vs. 3.60 D for 2). This further enhancement of the S0 state dipole moment is assigned to be a consequence of the π-donating effect of the lone-pair electrons on the N-atoms (n(N)) towards the ring, which acts stabilizing in the S0 state. However, when 3 is excited to the S1 state the electron density is opposed in the fulvene moiety, and π-donation towards the ring is no longer possible. Thus, the large change in dipole moment (|Δμge,S(3)| = 10.72 D) is a consequence of the loss of the ability of the N-atoms to donate π-electron density towards the ring. Moreover, the interaction between n(N) and the negative charge on the exocyclic C-atom will act destabilizing on the S1 state. Additionally, as for 2, the interaction between the π-electrons and n(Cl) will stabilize the S1 state and destabilize the S0 state. However, as the n(Cl) and n(N) occupies 3p and 2p type orbitals, respectively, it can be argued that the interaction between lone-pairs and the π-electrons at the fulvene moiety is greatest for the N-atoms. The excitation energy of 3 should therefore increase when compared to 1 and 2, and this is indeed observed. However, in n-heptane, cyclohexane and toluene the excitation energy of 3 is similar to that of 1. As the S0 state of 3 is the most polar state it is predicted that this state is most influenced by solvent polarity. Thus, in non-polar solvents the S0 state of 3 is destabilized, which results in excitation energies comparable with those of 1.

As for the S0 state dipole moment the σ-inductive effect of the substituents influences the magnitude of the S1 state dipole moments. Comparison of the magnitudes of the S1 state dipole moments gives: |μe,S(1)| > |μe,S(3)| ≈ |μe,S(2)|. For 1 there is no opposing σ-inductive effect present in the S1 state as for 2 and 3, and therefore |μe,S(1)| achieves the largest value. The difference between |μe,S(3)| and |μe,S(2)| (1.80 D vs. 1.48 D) can be explained by the presence of nitrogen atoms in 3 which act σ-withdrawing and thus enhances the S1 state dipole moment of 3 when compared to 2.

On a final note, according to the present work the change in the electron density distributions of 1–3 is less affected upon excitation to the T1 state, as the calculated T1 state dipole moments (μe,T, Table 4) of 1–3 are found to be reversed only for 1. It has been argued that triplet states have larger biradicaloid character, when compared to excited singlet states, which conversely have a larger ionic character.64 This could explain why the T1 states of 1–3 have less change in the electron density distribution when compared to the S1 state, and thus might be more influenced by excited state aromaticity.

Conclusion

The examination of the S1 state dipole moments of different fulvenes using solvatochromy and calculated S0 state dipole moments revealed reversals of the dipole moments upon excitation to the S1 states. The oppositely polarized electron density distributions, which dominate the S0 and S1 states for 1–3, respectively, indicate that cyclic delocalized arrangement of 4n π-electrons are favorable in the S1 state and thereby supports that the π-electron counting rules for S0 state aromaticity are reversed in the S1 state, when compared to the S0 state.

A cooperative π-polarization effect between the N-atoms and the fulvene moiety in 3 has an extensive effect on the S0 state dipole moment, but the effect is lost upon excitation. The cooperative π-polarization effect between the Cl-atoms and the fulvene moiety in the S1 state is minor, but the presence of the Cl-atoms affects the excitation energy of the fulvene moiety. This can indicate that better π-donating groups should be used as substituents on the ring before a cooperative effect can be achieved upon excitation. It would therefore be desirable to investigate fulvenes substituted with better π-electron donors on the ring to see whether a cooperative effect can be achieved upon excitation. Currently, the selection of fulvenes with such substituents is very sparse and further synthetic development of substituted fulvenes is needed. Future spectroscopic and theoretical investigations could also consider exocyclic-substituted heptafulvenes, which should behave opposite to pentafulvenes, i.e., π-electron withdrawing substituents would presumably influence the S0 state, while π-electron-donating substituents would influence the S1 state.

It was not possible to extract information about the direction and magnitude of the S1 state dipole moment of 4, due to an inferior description of solvent–solute interactions by the dielectric continuum model. Therefore, it was not possible to elucidate whether a cooperative π-polarizing effect of the π-accepting cyano-groups is present in the S1 state to further enhance the S1 state dipole moment of fulvenes.

Wan and co-workers have proven excited state aromaticity to be a valuable tool to predict and explain photochemistry of different organic compounds.31–46 The present work contributes to further and deeper understanding of how excited state aromaticity can be used as a tool for controlling the excited state energies and to predict electron density distributions in excited states. Hopefully, it will stimulate further investigations on the utilization of excited state aromaticity as a tool for predicting excited state photophysical properties of organic molecules.
